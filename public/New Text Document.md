If Nuclear Fusion Is So Perfect, Why Isn't It Our Energy Source?

Nuclear fusion is often hailed as humanity's "holy grail" of energy for good reason; virtually unlimited, clean and theoretically flawless. And yet, today, not a usable watt of power comes from nuclear fusion. What's holding us back from utilising this powerful energy source?

To understand why nuclear fusion is so elusive, let's first be clear on what it really is. Unlike with nuclear fission found in today's nuclear power plants, instead of splitting a larger atom into smaller ones, fusion smashes two atoms together. Releasing staggering amounts of energy during the process, it's more efficient and environment-friendly than nuclear fission.

The appeal of fusion is pretty damn high. Compared to fission, it uses bountiful elements like hydrogen, generates virtually zero long-term waste and is clean.
If perfected, nuclear energy would provide endless energy for millenia.

Okay, great! Why aren't we ditching all our solar panels and dino-jelly to move into nuclear fusion? The challenge lies not in the theory, we've got that part covered. It's in its execution.

Nuclear fusion is one of the most complex processes known to humanity and requires numerous conditions to be met in order to work. I've found that the challenges diverge into three main categories: Environment, Sustainability and Energy Input Versus Output.
• The Environment

If you want to recreate fusion, which powers a star, you're either going to need a star or mimic the conditions inside one. For reference, the sun's core boasts immense gravitational pressure (250 billion times that of Earth) and a temperature of 15 to 20 million kelvin. We have no way of matching that crushing pressure of the sun, so the only option is to crank the temperature up to a whopping 100-200 million kelvin.
• Sustainability

We've been able to create the environment multiple times. Now what's left is to sustain it. The longest sustained nuclear fusion reaction was a measly 1337 seconds by the WEST TOKAMAK in France. Excellent achievement by all means; nowhere near the days of continuous operation that plants would require, though.
• Energy Input Versus Output

What’s next is the issue with energy input and output. It’s arguably the most prominent hurdle fusion faces, since the whole point is to produce more energy than we put in. The heat required to heat the gases into plasma combined with confining it requires huge amounts of energy. While we’ve seen some exciting stuff lately with fusion reactors briefly making more power than they use, it’s not commercially viable at all.

Will fusion ever be the dominant source of energy? Most experts agree that it is not a matter of if, but when. Although fusion isn't one single breakthrough away from being the dominant source of energy, we're definitely making progress. Scientific extrapolation projects the first nuclear fusion reactor prototype to be made in 50 years, which would fuel a new revolution similar to the scaling of computers after transistors were invented.

Something about it being turtles all the way down never sits right with me.

Stephen Hawking opens 'A Brief History Of Time' with a little conversation between a young scientist and an old lady. After his lecture on how the earth moves around the sun, the lady berated him in the presence of a whole audience.
"The world is really a flat plate supported on the back of a giant tortoise."
The smug scientist asked her, "What's the tortoise standing on, then?", to which he got a reply that intrigues me more than anything in the lecture the man could've said that day.
"You're very clever, young man, very clever. But it's turtles all the way down!"
Hawking's Bottom Turtle: Logic
Hawking used this opener to write on the universe's nature and roots. However, he never questions the very existence of rules behind the universe. Mathematics governs reality, why though? We're fascinated by the physical nature of the universe itself, justifiably so, but we rarely go questioning rules.
As Eugene Wigner put it, math is 'unreasonably effective'. Something invented completely out of reason works for the universe too. We accept it without question, but my nerdy self thinks that it's ridiculous.
Order exists. Laws exist. Why?
We know next to nothing about the physical nature of the universe. Every answer raises infinitely more questions. The more we learn, the more there is to be learnt.
There's a peculiarity: Order exists, and the universe always seems to follow it. No matter how much we learn about quantum mechanics, general relativity or our own biology, they appear to bend to the rules. That's eerie to think about.
What governs logic?
Since the universe has order, logic underlies it. We think about logic using logic.
It's the turtle that humans cannot look under.
Questioning what governs logic is futile. The very act of questioning is grounded in logic. Any way of getting outside it traps you in the same bubble. What about concepts, then? Logic operates on concepts. 'If A then B' doesn't make sense without A or B, which fall under the idea of concepts.
The 'concept of concepts'
Concepts describe reality. It's how our mind processes 'things'.
But what is 'concept'? To explain it, you'd have to use concepts. Every philosophical definition of concept is a concept in and of itself. They're how you think, making it another bubble that you're a prisoner of.
If both pillars of cognition are inescapable cognitively, then the entirety of human understanding is built on the turtle of cognition that stands on itself. It's turtles all the way down, up and sideways.
If you ever wanted to break mentally, try to step outside cognition.
